Preamble

What this is

You have eight compulsions. They share one mechanism: you feel discomfort, you perform a behavior that removes it temporarily, your brain learns the behavior works, the threshold drops, the behaviors expand. The content looks responsible — checking, researching, optimizing, planning, building. That is exactly why they have lasted this long. They feel like work.

This plan treats all eight as the same operant problem. The surface varies. The intervention does not. You expose yourself to the discomfort, you do not perform the compulsion, you watch the discomfort peak and pass without the behavior, your brain learns the discomfort is survivable. You repeat this until the threshold rises and the behaviors shrink.

The ADHD-adjacent layer means you are fighting two things at once: you are avoiding discomfort and seeking the reward these behaviors deliver. Knowing this does not make you exempt from the plan. It means you will need to follow it more strictly, not less, because motivation will feel more variable and the pull of the behaviors will be stronger than it would be for someone without that layer.

This is not a suggestion document. Every rule below is a rule. When you feel like breaking one, that feeling is the compulsion — not a sign the rule is wrong.

§0 — The One Rule

The rule that governs all eight

Core Rule — Non-Negotiable

If it does not produce a deliverable, it is avoidance.

Checking is not producing. Researching is not producing. Optimizing is not producing.
Planning, organizing, prompting AI, building tools — none of it is producing.
You may do these things. In their designated time slots. Not before producing.

Before every session, answer one question in writing: "What will I have produced when this session ends, and in what form?" If you cannot answer it in one sentence, you do not start the session. You sit with the blankness until you can.

"The CBT question is not whether this behavior is thesis-related or work-related. It is whether it is producing output, or reducing anxiety."
§1 — Daily Structure

The non-negotiable daily frame

This structure is a stimulus-control intervention. Its purpose is to make avoidance physically harder by removing ambient permission for it. It is not flexible until you have followed it for 21 consecutive days.

Hard limits on the daily structure Stop
  • Do not check messages, email, or notifications before completing your morning intention statement.
  • Do not begin any research or AI use before completing at least one production block.
  • Do not carry the AI / research window beyond 30 minutes on any day. If you do, it closes.
  • Do not work after your declared end-of-day time. The shutdown ritual is non-negotiable even when the work feels unfinished.
  • Do not skip the end-of-day log. Without it, the behavioral-experiment data does not accumulate and the plan has no learning mechanism.
§2 — Compulsion 1 (Primary)

Compulsive checking

1
Compulsive Checking
CBT concept: Checking / reassurance-seeking / safety behavior · Protocol: ERP + Theory A/B
Primary · Highest frequency
What this is

Checking temporarily removes doubt. Your brain has learned that checking makes the discomfort go away. Research by van den Hout & Kindt shows that repeated checking actually degrades memory confidence — meaning every check you perform makes the next urge more likely, not less. You are not solving a problem. You are feeding a cycle.

The function it serves

You check because something feels unresolved, wrong, or uncertain. The check provides 60–90 seconds of relief. Then the doubt returns — usually stronger. The behavior that promises certainty is the behavior that destroys it.

Hard rules Stop
  • One-look rule: everything — every document, every sent message, every locked door — gets looked at exactly once. You look, you move on.
  • Do not re-read sent messages. Ever. After sending, the window closes.
  • Do not re-check completed work before starting a new block. Completed means completed.
  • Do not seek reassurance from other people as a substitute (asking a colleague "does this look right?" compulsively is the same behavior in social form).
⚡ When the urge hits — ERP sequence
1Name it out loud or in writing: "Checking urge. This is a compulsion, not a need."
2Set a 10-minute timer. Do not perform the check during this window.
3Rate your anxiety: 0–10 now. Rate again at 5 min. Watch it move without acting on it.
4When the timer ends: if the urge has not resolved, extend by 10 minutes. Most urges resolve without the check. That is the data.
5Log: urge fired / resisted Y/N / peak anxiety rating / actual outcome.
Weekly behavioral experiment

Pick one checking behavior. Leave it completely unchecked for 7 days. Complete before starting.

§3 — Compulsion 2 (Primary)

Compulsive optimizing

2
Compulsive Optimizing
CBT concept: Perfectionistic overcontrol · Protocol: Clinical perfectionism CBT (Shafran/Egan/Wade)
Primary · High cost
What this is

Reworking structure, renaming headings, adjusting outlines, refining themes — these are not production. They are the behavior of someone who will not commit to imperfect prose. The structure feels unstable. You optimize it instead of writing through it, and the structure becomes the problem you work on instead of the work itself.

The function it serves

Optimizing avoids the moment of exposure: producing something imperfect, in writing, that can be seen and judged. While you adjust the outline, you are never failing. This is the exact mechanism perfectionistic overcontrol maintains.

Hard rules Stop
  • Structure gets 5 minutes at session start. When the timer ends, the structure is locked. Write through it as-is.
  • Do not rename, reorder, or restructure anything during a production block. Bracketed notes ([restructure later?]) are permitted; acting on them is not.
  • No restructuring until the draft of a section exists. The draft comes first. Every time.
  • Define "good enough" in behavioral terms before writing. Write it down. Emotional readiness is not a criterion.
Good-enough criteria — fill this in before each session Required

This section is complete when it has: 1 claim. 2–3 pieces of evidence. 1 interpretive paragraph. 1 link to the central question. Bracketed gaps permitted. It is not complete when it feels complete. It is complete when those behavioral criteria are met.

  • Not: "feels theoretically secure." Not: "I would be comfortable showing this." Not: "I've checked all sources."
  • Yes: "claim stated. evidence cited. paragraph exists. link made." That is the finish line.
⚡ When the restructuring urge hits
1Name it: "Restructuring urge. This is optimizing, not producing."
2Write one bracketed note in the document: [RESTRUCTURE IDEA: ___]. Do not act on it.
3Return to the sentence you were on. Type the next word. That is the only required action.
4Disputation check: "Would I hold a colleague to this structural standard at draft stage?" If no — keep writing.

Resource: CCI Perfectionism in Perspective, Modules 5–6 · cci.health.wa.gov.au | Shafran, Egan & Wade, Overcoming Perfectionism (2018)

§4 — Compulsion 3 (Primary)

Compulsive researching

3
Compulsive Researching
CBT concept: Intolerance of uncertainty / preparatory avoidance · Protocol: CBT-IU (Dugas/Robichaud)
Primary · Very high cost
What this is

"One more source" is not a research need. It is an anxiety-reduction behavior. CBT-IU (Behavioral Experiments for Intolerance of Uncertainty, Hebert & Dugas 2019) treats information-seeking as a safety behavior that lowers distress short-term and reinforces the catastrophic belief: "I cannot act until I am certain." More research does not produce certainty. It produces more research.

The function it serves

Reading makes you feel competent without requiring you to produce. Researching seeks epistemic certainty you cannot actually obtain. The CBT move is not to stop researching permanently — it is to break the belief that certainty must precede action, by acting without it and recording what actually happens.

Hard rules Stop
  • Before any research: write the specific question in one sentence. If you cannot write it in one sentence, you are not ready to research — you are anxious.
  • Source cap: maximum 3 sources per sub-claim. When the cap is reached, the research is done.
  • 25-minute hard timebox per research session. Timer set before opening any browser or document.
  • When the timer ends, you write with what you have. Brackets for gaps are permitted — [CITE] is fine. Missing a source is not a crisis. That is the experiment.
  • Research parking lot: any "one more source" urge during a production block goes onto the paper list. You do not act on it during the block.
Triage: real problem or hypothetical worry? Apply to every urge
  • Current, real, actionable problem (there is a specific gap in a specific claim) → structured 25-min research with source cap. Write immediately after.
  • Hypothetical "what if" (I might be missing something / what if there's a better framing) → research will not help. Park it. Continue writing. Return in weekly review.

§5 — Compulsion 4 (Primary)

Compulsive productivity

4
Compulsive Productivity
CBT concept: Productive procrastination · Protocol: Behavioral Activation + CCI Procrastination
Primary · Disguised as work
What this is

Planning, organizing, tracking, filing, formatting — these feel like work and produce nothing. Pychyl's research frames procrastination as short-term mood repair: you substitute the difficult task with a lower-priority one, get relief, and the brain records the substitution as successful. The CCI Procrastination workbook names this explicitly: doing lower-priority tasks instead of the priority task is procrastination, regardless of how productive it looks.

The function it serves

Productivity activities create the feeling of progress without exposing you to the discomfort of actual production. They are particularly sticky for your pattern because the ADHD-adjacent reward-seeking mechanism makes task-switching genuinely pleasurable. You are not being lazy. You are being very effective at avoidance.

Hard rules Stop
  • When you notice yourself planning, organizing, filing, or formatting during production time: stop. Name it out loud: "This is avoidance, not work."
  • Inbox zero, notifications, filing, task-list maintenance — none of these happen during production blocks. They have no designated slot; they happen after all production blocks are complete, if at all.
  • The smallest next physical action: when resistance hits, ask only "what is the next word / line / sentence?" Never "what does this section need?"
TRAP → TRAC — apply to every avoidance substitution Daily log
  • Trigger — what feeling started it? Response — what did you do instead? Avoidance Pattern — the substitution cycle.
  • Trigger (same) → Replacement Alternative Coping — implement intention: "IF dread when I open the doc → THEN I write one imperfect paragraph for 25 minutes."

Resource: CCI Put Off Procrastinating, Modules 5–6 · cci.health.wa.gov.au | Sirois & Pychyl, Procrastination, Health & Wellbeing (2016)

⚡ When the avoidance substitution begins
1Stop. Name it: "This is productive procrastination."
2Stay in front of the document for 60 seconds without doing anything. Observe the dread without acting on it.
3Deploy implementation intention: write one bad sentence. Not a good one. One sentence, in any condition.
4End each block with a self-compassionate close: "That was hard. I did it." — not to soften the rules, but because self-criticism drives the next avoidance cycle. (Sirois, 2014)
§6 — Compulsion 5 (Primary)

Compulsive problem-solving

5
Compulsive Problem-Solving
CBT concept: Rumination / over-analysis · Protocol: Rumination-Focused CBT (Watkins) + MCT (Wells)
Primary · High duration
What this is

Watkins & Moulds (2005) showed the mechanism: abstract/evaluative thinking ("why is this not working?" "what does this mean?") impairs problem-solving. Concrete/process-focused thinking ("what is the next step?") improves it. When you spin on a problem rather than writing your way through it, you are choosing the mode of thinking that makes it worse, not better.

The function it serves

Mental problem-spinning keeps the problem safely unresolved and in your control. The moment you write through it — even imperfectly — it becomes testable, falsifiable, and judgeable. Rumination keeps the problem mental rather than textual, which is exactly the avoidance mechanism.

Hard rules Stop
  • Why → How shift: the moment you notice "why"-mode thinking, reformulate: "What specifically is the next step about this, by when?" Do not continue in "why" mode.
  • 60-second externalization rule: any problem you are spinning on gets typed as one sentence in a document within 60 seconds. One sentence: situation. One sentence: next action. If you cannot write it in two sentences, the problem is too abstract — that is the symptom, not the problem.
  • Scheduled Thinking Time only: 30 minutes, at a desk, with the CCI Active Coping worksheet on paper. Problems that arise outside this slot are parked on a card with: "I'll work on this at [time]."
  • No problem-spinning in the shower, in transit, or in bed. These are not thinking contexts. If an idea appears, write it in one sentence and move on.
Thinking Time protocol — 30 min, scheduled, once daily Structured
  • Sit at a desk. Paper and pen only — no screens, no AI.
  • Use the CCI Active Coping 6-step structure: Define concretely → Brainstorm ≥4 options → Evaluate → Choose with: action + who + when → Implement → Evaluate.
  • If the problem cannot be defined in one concrete sentence, it is not ready for Thinking Time. Write "not ready yet" and close the notebook.
  • If no action is decidable after 30 minutes, the problem is misformulated. Rewrite the definition, or convert it to a behavioral experiment with a concrete test.

Resource: CCI Active Coping worksheet · CCI Working with Worry and Rumination manual · Watkins, Rumination-Focused CBT (Guilford 2016)

§7 — Compulsion 6 (Primary)

Compulsive availability

6
Compulsive Availability
CBT concept: Workaholic cognition / poor disengagement · Protocol: Recovery Experiences (Sonnentag) + stimulus control
Primary · Prevents recovery
What this is

Staying mentally attached to work after the work day ends is not conscientiousness. Bakker, Demerouti, Oerlemans & Sonnentag (2013) found that work-related activity in the evening is specifically more harmful for workaholics than for others, while physical and social activities are specifically more protective. You cannot recover by staying available. You recover by fully disengaging.

The function it serves

Being available prevents the disconfirming experience that nothing catastrophic happens when you disconnect. Sonnentag & Fritz's Recovery Experience Questionnaire identifies four protective experiences: psychological detachment, relaxation, mastery, and control — none of which can occur while you remain cognitively attached to work.

Hard rules Stop
  • Hard stop time: [you define this — write it here: ___:___ ]. Non-negotiable. The shutdown ritual begins at this time regardless of where you are in the work.
  • No email, Slack, or work messages after the stop time. Logged out on phone. Not muted — logged out.
  • No work-adjacent content after stop time: no work podcasts, no "light reading" that is actually research, no tool browsing, no AI use.
  • Phone physically in another room from 9pm until after your morning intention statement.
Shutdown ritual — every day, at stop time Non-negotiable
  • Brain dump: open loops and tomorrow's one-sentence intention — onto paper, not into AI or task manager.
  • Physical close: laptop closed in a drawer. Email and Slack signed out on phone. Laptop and phone separated.
  • Verbal cue out loud: "Work is done for today." This sounds absurd. Do it anyway — it functions as a behavioral boundary marker.
  • Simulated commute: 15–20 minute walk or shower, no work content, no earbuds.
  • One scheduled non-work activity from Sonnentag's four: detachment / relaxation / mastery (non-work skill) / control (leisure choice). Physical and social activities specifically.
⚡ When the "just check" urge hits in the evening
1Defuse: "I'm noticing the thought that I should check. That thought is not an instruction."
2Opposite action: do not check. Stay engaged with the chosen activity.
3Park the thought on a carry-over card: one sentence, checked tomorrow in the first 15-minute block. Do not act on it tonight.
4Run the behavioral experiment: you predicted something bad would happen if you didn't check. Check tomorrow whether it did. It almost certainly did not. Log it.
§8 — Compulsion 7 (Secondary)

AI / tool loop dependence

7
AI / Tool Loop Dependence
CBT concept: Reassurance-seeking / externalized judgment · Protocol: ERP + Theory A/B (reassurance-seeking variant)
Secondary · Uniquely disguised
What this is

Using AI to "compare framings," "check if this is right," or "generate options before deciding" is functionally identical to asking a person for reassurance. It is not a productivity tool in those moments. It is a machine that never tires of providing relief, never refuses, generates more options (which increases uncertainty, which drives more prompting), and is available at 2am. The IOCDF defines reassurance-seeking as: repetitive, anxiety-driven, demanding certainty. Apply that definition honestly to your AI use.

The function it serves

AI use outsources the discomfort of making a decision under uncertainty. It externalizes your judgment rather than building tolerance for imperfect judgment. Every time you prompt before deciding, you confirm the meta-belief: "I cannot trust my own assessment." That belief gets stronger, not weaker, with use.

The functional analysis you must run on every AI use Before every prompt

The IOCDF distinction, applied directly:

  • Information use (permitted): one-time, accepts the answer, specific factual question, would a single qualified human give the same answer once. → Permitted in the AI window.
  • Reassurance seeking (compulsion): repetitive or rephrased, anxiety-driven, demands certainty, unanswerable, compares two AI responses for "another opinion." → Stop. Do not submit. Surf the urge.
Hard rules Stop
  • AI is available in one 30-minute window per day. Outside that window, it is logged out. Not minimized — logged out.
  • One-Ask Rule: before any prompt, write in your notebook: (a) the question in one sentence, (b) the feeling you are trying to reduce, (c) is this answerable and one-time? If anxiety-driven — do not submit.
  • Never re-ask the same question with different wording. If you rephrase, you are seeking reassurance, not information. Close the window.
  • Never compare two AI responses. "Let me ask Claude and then check GPT" is the same compulsion in two browser tabs.
  • No AI use for decisions about your own work. Produce first. If you want AI feedback after producing, that is a different function — permitted in the window, once per section.
⚡ When the prompting urge hits outside the window
1Write the question in the notebook. That is enough for now.
2Rate the anxiety: 0–10. Set a 10-minute timer. Do not open AI.
3Ask: "What is the worst that happens if I make this decision with my own judgment right now?" Write the answer. Then make the decision without prompting.
4Alternative: contact a human and say "I'm anxious about X — I don't need you to fix it, I need company while I sit with it." Support without certainty. (Halldorsson & Salkovskis, 2017)

§9 — Compulsion 8 (Secondary)

Compulsive tool-building

8
Compulsive Tool-Building
CBT concept: Preparatory avoidance / optimization ritual · Protocol: ACT workability + CCI Procrastination
Secondary · Feels like investment
What this is

Building tools, systems, automations, integrations, note templates, and workflows is preparatory avoidance. It is the behavior of improving the conditions for work while not doing the work. ACT's "workability" test applies cleanly: does this directly produce the deliverable, or is it about producing the deliverable? The system is never finished because the system is not the point — the discomfort of starting is the point.

The function it serves

Russ Harris's DOTS audit names the implicit rule that drives this: "The next system will fix it." Every system you built in the last two years reduced anxiety temporarily. None of them solved the underlying avoidance. The next one won't either. Building gives the sensation of progress without requiring output.

Hard rules Stop
  • 30-day tool freeze, starting today. No new tools, plug-ins, templates, integrations, or system changes for 30 days. Capture everything in a plain text file or a single notebook.
  • Tool-building has one designated slot: Fridays, 4:00–5:00pm. Outside that slot, all tool urges go onto the Yak List on paper.
  • The workability test applies before every tool session: "Can I name two specific future deliverables this directly serves?" If no — the tool is not built.
  • No tool-building until at least two production blocks have been completed that day.
DOTS audit — do this once, this week

List every tool, system, or workflow you adopted in the past 24 months. Against each: (a) did it permanently eliminate the discomfort that drove you to build it? (b) What did it cost in time and foregone production? The pattern you will see is the data against the meta-belief "the next system will fix it."

§11 — Weekly Review

The weekly review

Fridays. 30 minutes. Paper only. Not in the tool-building window — before it. This is the only time you look at patterns across the week. It is also the only time you consult the Yak List and Research Parking Lot.

Weekly review questions

Which compulsion fired most frequently this week?

What predictions did I make that were disconfirmed? (The catastrophes that did not happen.)

Which rules did I break? What was the urge that preceded each breach?

§12 — When Everything Collapses

Emergency protocol

There will be days when every compulsion fires at once and you follow none of the rules. This is not failure — it is an OCD-spectrum pattern under stress, which behaves exactly like this. The protocol for those days is not to restart the entire plan. It is to do one thing.

⚠ Emergency sequence — in order, stop when stable
  1. Name what is happening. Out loud: "Every compulsion is firing and I am in avoidance mode." This activates prefrontal labeling and reduces amygdala reactivity. (Lieberman et al., 2007)
  2. Do not try to fix everything. Pick the one compulsion with the highest current urge. Apply only that section's protocol. One urge, one intervention.
  3. Set a 5-minute timer and produce one sentence of the primary deliverable. One sentence. In any condition. This is the behavioral experiment. The specific sentence does not matter.
  4. Log what happened. What fired. What you resisted. What you did not. One sentence each. This converts the collapse into data.
  5. Apply the self-compassionate close. Not to excuse the breach — to prevent the shame spiral that drives the next avoidance cycle. "That was a hard day. The plan continues tomorrow."
  6. Do not rebuild, redesign, or restructure the plan. The urge to redesign the system after a bad day is Compulsion 8 in disguise. The plan stands as written.

One honest caveat. This plan is evidence-based and self-directed. Self-directed ERP is significantly harder than therapist-administered ERP. If these compulsions are materially impairing your functioning — output, relationships, sleep, health — a therapist trained in ERP and OCD-spectrum work would accelerate this considerably. The plan above is not a replacement for that; it is the best available without it.

The ADHD-adjacent layer is also worth separate attention. If there is genuine ADHD underneath this pattern, that dysregulation can undermine CBT gains. Not make them impossible — but harder to sustain without addressing that layer. This is not a reason to delay starting the plan. It is a reason to be honest when the gains plateau.
