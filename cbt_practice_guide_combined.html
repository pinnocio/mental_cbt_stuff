Personal CBT Practice Plan · Self-Directed

Eight Compulsions.
One Rule.

A behavioral contract — not reading material.

You have eight compulsions. They share one mechanism: discomfort appears, you perform a behavior that removes it temporarily, your brain learns the behavior works, the threshold drops, and the behaviors expand. The content looks responsible — checking, researching, optimizing, planning, building, prompting AI. That is exactly why they have lasted this long. They feel like work.

This plan treats all eight as the same operant problem. You expose yourself to the discomfort, you do not perform the compulsion, you watch the discomfort peak and pass without the behavior, your brain learns the discomfort is survivable. You repeat until the threshold rises and the behaviors shrink.

The ADHD-adjacent layer means you are fighting two things at once: avoiding discomfort and seeking the reward these behaviors deliver. That means you will need to follow the rules more strictly, not less — because motivation will be more variable and the pull will be stronger.

§0 — Non-Negotiable

The One Rule

Core Rule — Governs All Eight Compulsions

If it does not produce a deliverable, it is avoidance.

Checking is not producing. Researching is not producing. Optimizing is not producing. Planning, organizing, prompting AI, building tools — none of it is producing. You may do these things — in their designated time slots, not before producing.

Before every session — write this answer

"What will I have produced when this session ends, and in what form?"

If you cannot answer it in one sentence, you do not start the session. You sit with the blankness until you can. The CBT question is not whether the behavior is work-related. It is whether it is producing output, or reducing anxiety.

The Practical CBT Shape

Every intervention follows the same structure regardless of which compulsion fires:

  1. Notice the trigger — what discomfort or urge appeared.
  2. Write the prediction — what you believe will go wrong if you don't perform the compulsion.
  3. Block or delay the compulsion — follow that section's hard rules.
  4. Do the smallest direct-output action available.
  5. Measure what actually happened — compare to the prediction.
  6. Repeat until the threat prediction weakens.

Self-talk is useful only if it helps you do the behavior while the urge is still present. The goal is not to make the discomfort disappear before acting. The goal is to act while it is present.

§1 — Stimulus Control

Daily Structure

This structure is a stimulus-control intervention. Its purpose is to make avoidance physically harder by removing ambient permission for it. It is not flexible until you have followed it for 21 consecutive days.

Morning
≤ 5 min
Intention statement Write one sentence: "Today I will produce [specific output] by [time]." No other planning. No checking messages first. This is the first thing you do.
Block 1
50 min
Mode-locked production One window open: the work document. Allowed: existing notes. Forbidden: AI, browser, email, tools, restructuring. Output in whatever condition it emerges.
Break
10 min
Physical break only No screens. No phone. No "quick checks." Walk, water, stretch. If you check anything during this break, the block resets.
Block 2
50 min
Mode-locked production Same rules as Block 1. If research is genuinely necessary, park the specific question on paper with a bracket [CHECK LATER] and keep producing.
Midday
30 min max
AI + Research window The only permitted slot for AI use and research. Hard stop at 30 minutes by timer. If you exceed it, the window closes for the rest of the day. Governed by §8 rules.
Block 3
50 min
Mode-locked production Same rules as Blocks 1–2. The output from this block is the measurable deliverable against your morning intention.
End of day
10 min
Shutdown ritual + daily log Brain dump of open loops onto paper (not into AI). Write tomorrow's one-sentence intention. Close laptop. Phone away. Walk or shower without content. Work is done.
Hard Limits — No Exceptions
  • Do not check messages or notifications before completing your morning intention statement.
  • Do not begin any research or AI use before completing at least one production block.
  • Do not carry the AI / research window beyond 30 minutes. If you do, it closes for the day.
  • Do not work after your declared end-of-day time. The shutdown ritual is non-negotiable even when the work feels unfinished.
  • Do not skip the end-of-day log. Without it, the behavioral-experiment data does not accumulate and the plan has no learning mechanism.
§2 — Compulsion 1 · ERP + Theory A/B

Compulsive Checking Primary

Theory A vs Theory B

Theory A (the anxiety belief): "The problem is that something bad might happen if I don't check." This belief makes checking feel necessary and protective.

Theory B (the CBT reframe): "The problem is that I believe something bad will happen — and I keep checking to manage that belief, which strengthens it." Under Theory B, the check is never solving the real problem; it is maintaining it. The intervention is to act as if Theory B is true — meaning the check is optional, the discomfort is survivable, and the evidence accumulates that nothing required checking after all.

What this is

Research by van den Hout & Kindt shows that repeated checking actually degrades memory confidence — meaning every check you perform makes the next urge more likely, not less. You are not solving a problem. You are feeding a cycle.

You check because something feels unresolved, wrong, or uncertain. The check provides 60–90 seconds of relief. Then the doubt returns — usually stronger. The behavior that promises certainty is the behavior that destroys it.

Hard Rules
  • One-look rule: everything — every document, every sent message, every locked door — gets one look. You look, you move on.
  • Do not re-read sent messages. After sending, the window closes.
  • Do not re-check completed work before starting a new block. Completed means completed.
  • Do not seek social reassurance as a substitute — asking a colleague "does this look right?" compulsively is the same behavior in social form.

⚡ When the urge hits — ERP sequence

Step 1Name it out loud or in writing: "Checking urge. This is a compulsion, not a need."
Step 2Set a 10-minute timer. Do not perform the check during this window.
Step 3Rate anxiety 0–10 now. Rate again at 5 min. Watch it move without acting on it.
Step 4When the timer ends: if the urge has not resolved, extend by 10 minutes. Most urges resolve without the check. That is the data.
Step 5Log: urge fired / resisted Y/N / peak anxiety rating / actual outcome.

§3 — Compulsion 2 · Clinical Perfectionism CBT

Compulsive Optimizing Primary · High cost

What this is

Compulsive optimizing is the repeated urge to improve the conditions, structure, wording, system, tool, process, or plan before acting. It often looks intelligent and responsible. That is why it is sticky. Core definition: compulsive optimizing is not "liking things to be good." It is the pattern where improving the setup repeatedly delays, displaces, or dilutes the actual output.

Healthy optimization
  • Solves a specific bottleneck
  • Has a clear stopping point
  • Leads directly to an output
  • Proportionate to the task
  • Can be postponed without panic
Compulsive optimization
  • Expands as you work on it
  • Feels urgent even when unnecessary
  • Delays the real task
  • Creates temporary relief
  • Returns quickly: "just one more improvement"

Common forms — what it looks like across contexts

ContextCompulsive optimizing looks likeOutput being avoided
Writing / academicReworking theme names, chapter structure, note categories, literature maps.Writing rough prose and making provisional claims.
Work projectRebuilding the plan, slide structure, project board, documentation format.Sending the draft, making the decision, shipping the deliverable.
Software / codingRefactoring architecture, improving naming, changing frameworks, adding abstractions.Implementing the feature, testing the bug fix, releasing the version.
Personal productivitySwitching apps, redesigning tags, building dashboards, changing routines.Doing the unpleasant task already on the list.
Health / habitsCreating the perfect workout plan, tracker, diet protocol, supplement stack.Doing today's workout or cooking the next meal.
Creative workChoosing tools, templates, palettes, structure, references, aesthetic direction.Making the draft, sketch, scene, page, or prototype.

CBT formulation

Compulsive optimizing functions as a combination of perfectionistic safety behavior, avoidance, and short-term anxiety regulation. It is maintained because it gives relief now while preventing you from learning that imperfect action is survivable.

Trigger — "This is not good enough / not clear enough / not ready."
↓
Threat prediction — "If I act now, the result will be bad, judged, inefficient, wrong, embarrassing, or wasteful."
↓
Safety behavior — optimize the structure, tool, wording, plan, system, or workflow.
↓
Short-term effect — relief, control, stimulation, feeling of progress.
↓
Long-term effect — less tolerance for imperfect action, more dependence on optimizing, less output.

CBT target: not to stop improving things forever. The target is to stop using optimization as the gatekeeper before action.

Operational rules — choose one or two to start

  1. Output before optimization. Produce a rough output before improving the system around it. Write the paragraph before renaming the theme. Ship the draft before redesigning the template.
  2. Optimization must have a ticket. You may optimize only if you can write a specific problem ticket: "This exact bottleneck prevented this exact output." Vague tickets like "make the system better" are not permitted.
  3. Time-box, then act. Give optimization a short window: 5, 10, or 15 minutes. When the timer ends, act with the current version. No emotional readiness required.
  4. No mid-flight redesign. Once a production block starts, no changing the structure, tool, framework, theme name, template, or plan. Capture ideas in brackets or on paper. Keep producing.
  5. Revise from output. You are allowed to improve the structure after a draft exists. The draft becomes evidence. Before the draft, most optimization is speculation.
  6. Use behavioral "good enough" criteria. Define "good enough" before starting. Do not use feelings as the finish line. If it meets the behavioral criteria, it is done.
Hard Rules
  • Structure gets 5 minutes at session start. When the timer ends, the structure is locked. Write through it as-is.
  • Do not rename, reorder, or restructure anything during a production block. Bracketed notes ([restructure later?]) are permitted; acting on them is not.
  • No restructuring until a draft of the section exists. Draft comes first. Every time.
  • No mid-flight tool, theme, or template changes during an output block.
  • Systemic optimization (rebuild workflow, redesign folders, switch apps, create new dashboard) is never allowed before output.

TaskBad criterionBehavioral criterion
Writing (section/paragraph)"It feels clear and right."One claim, one explanation, one piece of evidence or placeholder, one next-step note.
Work deliverable"The whole system feels organized."Owner, next action, deadline, and first deliverable are specified.

⚡ 10-minute anti-optimization sequence

Use this when you are already inside the urge. It does not ask you to convince yourself — it gives your body something specific to do.

Step 1Freeze the optimization. Hands off the outline, template, tool, theme name, plan, code structure, or system.
Step 2Write the urge as a ticket. One sentence only: "I want to optimize ___ because I fear ___."
Step 3Defer it visibly. Put it in the Optimization Parking Lot below.
Step 4Choose the smallest output action: one sentence, one line of code, one paragraph stub, one email draft, one messy slide.
Step 5Work 10 minutes with optimization blocked. Timer on. No structural changes.
Step 6Re-rate the urge 0–10 before and after.
Step 7Decide from evidence. After output exists, ask whether the optimization is still necessary.

Handling the physical urge

The urge to optimize often arrives as bodily discomfort: tension, pressure, agitation, dread, heat, restlessness, or a sharp sense that something is "wrong." Do not wait for it to disappear. If you wait to feel settled before acting, the urge has already become the gatekeeper.

1Pause hands. Stop touching the thing you want to optimize.
2Name the body sensation: "tight chest," "buzzing," "heat," "pressure," "restlessness."
3Rate intensity 0–10.
4Make room physically: unclench jaw, lower shoulders, exhale longer than inhale for three breaths.
5Do not negotiate with the urge. No debating whether optimization is justified for the next 10 minutes.
6Perform one output action: type the next sentence, line, claim, test, or email sentence.
7Re-rate after 10 minutes. The goal is not zero. The goal is movement without compulsion.
Intensity guidance

7/10 or lower: continue the exposure.
8–9/10: shrink the task, not the rule. One sentence instead of one paragraph.
Feels unsafe or overwhelming: stop the exposure, use grounding, and discuss pacing with a therapist. Do not use this guide as a substitute for clinical care.

Worked examples

Example A — Wanting to rename a theme or heading

Old behavior: spend 45 minutes generating alternatives, asking AI, reorganizing subthemes, then feel too drained to write.

New behavior:

  1. Write: "I want to rename this because I fear the current name misrepresents the content."
  2. Add bracket: [Possible rename later]
  3. Lock current name for 25 minutes.
  4. Write 250 words under the current name.
  5. After writing, ask: did the name actually block the paragraph?
  6. If still needed, schedule a 10-minute naming review after prose exists.
Example B — Wanting to redesign notes, dashboard, or system

Old behavior: open Notion/Obsidian/Sheets, restructure databases, add tags, create views, then call it progress.

New behavior:

  1. Write a tool ticket: "I want to redesign the dashboard because I fear I cannot see what to do next."
  2. Test the belief: can I define the next action without redesigning?
  3. Choose one output: "Write 200 words explaining X."
  4. Work 25 minutes using current notes as-is.
  5. Only if the messy notes directly prevented output, define a tiny fix — not "rebuild the whole system."
Example C — Wanting to refactor code before completing the feature

Old behavior: reorganize architecture, rename modules, improve abstractions, then never test the actual feature.

New behavior:

  1. Define output: "Main user path works once."
  2. Lock architecture for 45 minutes.
  3. Add TODOs instead of refactoring: // TODO: rename this after feature works
  4. Complete the feature path.
  5. Refactor only after: feature runs, one test exists, specific pain point is demonstrated.
Example D — Repeatedly rewriting a message because the tone is not perfect

Old behavior: edit for 30 minutes, ask AI for alternate versions, compare phrasing, delay sending.

New behavior:

  1. Good-enough criteria: clear recipient, clear ask, necessary context, deadline, polite enough.
  2. One draft. One 3-minute edit. Send.
  3. Exposure: send a polite but imperfect message and record whether the feared outcome happened.

Red flags — when optimization may actually be justified

Use this checklist before acting on an optimization urge
  • The current setup creates a real safety, legal, ethical, or privacy risk.
  • The output cannot proceed at all without the fix — not just more comfortably, but at all.
  • The same specific bottleneck has blocked output more than once.
  • The fix can be done in under 15 minutes and leads immediately back to output.
  • The optimization is scheduled, bounded, and not performed during an urge spike.

If only one item is true, be cautious. If the main reason is "I'll feel better once this is cleaner," it is the compulsion, not the task.

Shortest usable version — when you cannot think clearly

Crisis protocol — 6 steps
  1. Stop optimizing.
  2. Write the fear: "If I do not optimize ___, then ___."
  3. Put the optimization idea in the parking lot.
  4. Set 10 minutes.
  5. Produce one imperfect piece of output.
  6. Re-check whether optimization is still necessary after output exists.
§4 — Compulsion 3 · CBT-IU (Dugas / Robichaud)

Compulsive Researching Primary · Very high cost

What this is

"One more source" is not a research need. It is an anxiety-reduction behavior. CBT for intolerance of uncertainty treats information-seeking as a safety behavior that lowers distress short-term and reinforces the catastrophic belief: "I cannot act until I am certain." More research does not produce certainty. It produces more research.

Reading makes you feel competent without requiring you to produce. The CBT move is not to stop researching permanently — it is to break the belief that certainty must precede action, by acting without it and recording what actually happens.

Research through AI — a specific form

AI-assisted research is especially powerful because it feels targeted and efficient while actually expanding uncertainty. Recognize these surface-question / actual-function pairs:

Surface questionActual functionReplacement practice
"Is this paragraph okay?"ReassuranceSend to one bounded checklist: claim, evidence, transition.
"Did I miss anything?"Certainty seekingName one specific risk; check once after output exists.
"Can you verify this again?"Recursive checkingStop after one verification pass.
"Compare my answer to best practice."Perfectionistic benchmarkingUse minimum viable standard.

Open-ended vs bounded research

Open-ended — not allowed before output
  • "I need to understand this better."
  • "What are all the approaches?"
  • "What am I missing?"
  • "Find the best sources."
  • "How do people think about X generally?"
Bounded — allowed after output gap appears
  • "I need one source for this specific claim."
  • "I need the definition of this term."
  • "Check whether this claim is contradicted by Y."
  • "I need the deadline for this form."
  • "Find one example of Z."
Hard Rules
  • Before any research: write the specific question in one sentence. If you cannot write it in one sentence, you are anxious — not ready to research.
  • Source cap: maximum 3 sources per sub-claim. When the cap is reached, research is done.
  • 25-minute hard timebox per research session. Timer set before opening any browser or document.
  • When the timer ends, write with what you have. Brackets for gaps are permitted — [CITE] is fine. Missing a source is not a crisis.
  • Research parking lot: any "one more source" urge during a production block goes onto a paper list. Not acted on during the block.

Structured research stopping rule

  1. Before research: write the output gap in one sentence. If you cannot name it, the urge is anxiety, not a gap.
  2. During research: maximum 25 minutes, maximum 3 sources or 3 pages. No new branches unless they block the current output.
  3. After research: return immediately to the output. Write one sentence or make one change before any further research.

Triage: real problem or hypothetical worry?

Structured research — allowed

Current, real, actionable gap in a specific claim → 25-minute timebox with source cap → write immediately after.

Hypothetical worry — park it

"I might be missing something / what if there's a better framing" → research will not help. Park it. Continue producing. Return in weekly review.

Behavioral experiment — one-source repair

Prediction: "If I do not research broadly, I will miss something important."

Experiment: Use only one targeted source to repair one existing, named gap. Then return to output immediately.

Measure: Was broad research actually needed? Did one targeted repair move the task forward? Did broad curiosity try to take over?

§5 — Compulsion 4 · Behavioral Activation + Procrastination CBT

Compulsive Productivity Primary · Disguised as work

What this is

Planning, organizing, tracking, filing, formatting — these feel like work and produce nothing. Pychyl's research frames procrastination as short-term mood repair: you substitute the difficult task with a lower-priority one, get relief, and the brain records the substitution as successful. Doing lower-priority tasks instead of the priority task is procrastination, regardless of how productive it looks.

Productivity activities create the feeling of progress without exposing you to the discomfort of actual production. They are particularly sticky because the ADHD-adjacent reward-seeking mechanism makes task-switching genuinely pleasurable. You are not being lazy. You are being very effective at avoidance.

Trap signatures — recognize it fast

SignatureWhat it sounds likeWhat is happening
Task-list grooming"Let me clarify the tasks first."Planning is replacing starting.
Calendar reshuffling"I need a better schedule."Control-seeking is replacing exposure to the task.
Inbox clearing"I'll clear email so I can focus."Small completions are replacing hard progress.
Workspace cleaning"I can't work in this mess."Physical order is being used to avoid emotional disorder.
Tool / method research"There must be a better system."System improvement is replacing output.
Easy-task stacking"I'll do these quick things first."Completion dopamine is replacing meaningful work.
Meta-work"I need to rethink how I work."The work system has become the work.

Fast detection rule: if the behavior makes you feel more prepared but does not produce the target output, treat it as suspect during work-starting time.

Hard Rules
  • When you notice yourself planning, organizing, filing, or formatting during production time: stop. Name it out loud: "This is avoidance, not work."
  • Inbox zero, notifications, filing, task-list maintenance — none during production blocks. After all production blocks are complete, if at all.
  • The smallest next physical action: when resistance hits, ask only "what is the next word / line / sentence?" Never "what does this section need?"

The 7 + 25 protocol

This is the basic practice. It is a behavioral sequence, not a mantra.

  1. Pick one target output. It must be visible and concrete: paragraph, email draft, invoice sent, form submitted, slide drafted, phone call made.
  2. Set a 7-minute planning timer. Planning ends when the timer ends. You are not trying to feel ready. You are only identifying the first output.
  3. Write the first action as a physical behavior. Bad: "Work on project." Good: "Open the document and write the first rough paragraph under heading X."
  4. Set a 25-minute production timer. During this timer: only target-output behavior. No planning, improving, checking, cleaning, or optimizing.
  5. When avoidance appears, write it on a parking line — "Organize references later" — then immediately return to the target output.
  6. Record output, not feelings. "What exists now that did not exist before?"

7-minute planning — minute by minute

0–2 minChoose one deliverable. One concrete output — not a theme, not a project, not a goal.
2–5 minList the first three physical actions. "Open file → write heading → write first sentence."
5–7 minOpen the work surface. Get the file, document, or tool actually open.
Minute 7Start producing, even if the plan is incomplete. Incomplete plan is not an excuse to extend planning.

TRAP → TRAC — apply to every avoidance substitution

Use this to name the pattern when it happens, then construct the replacement response.

TRAP (the pattern)
  • Trigger — what feeling started it? (dread, boredom, shame, uncertainty)
  • Response — what did you do instead? (cleaned, planned, organized, opened AI)
  • Avoidance
  • Pattern — the substitution cycle repeats
TRAC (the replacement)
  • Trigger (same)
  • Replacement
  • Alternative
  • Coping — implement the intention: "IF dread when I open the doc → THEN I write one imperfect paragraph for 25 minutes."

Plain document rule (anti-system)

When tempted to create a system

Open the plainest possible tool instead: blank document, notebook page, basic text editor, simple email draft, paper. No system is allowed until seven plain-paper repetitions of actual output exist. Do not build a tracker for your CBT practice — that is Compulsion 8 in disguise.

§6 — Compulsion 5 · Rumination-Focused CBT + MCT (Wells)

Compulsive Problem-Solving Primary · High duration

What this is

Watkins & Moulds (2005) showed the mechanism: abstract/evaluative thinking ("why is this not working?" "what does this mean?") impairs problem-solving. Concrete/process-focused thinking ("what is the next step?") improves it. When you spin on a problem rather than writing your way through it, you are choosing the mode of thinking that makes it worse.

Mental problem-spinning keeps the problem safely unresolved and in your control. The moment you write through it — even imperfectly — it becomes testable, falsifiable, and judgeable. Rumination keeps the problem mental rather than textual, which is exactly the avoidance mechanism.

Hard Rules
  • Why → How shift: the moment you notice "why"-mode thinking, reformulate: "What specifically is the next step about this, by when?" Do not continue in "why" mode.
  • 60-second externalization rule: any problem you are spinning on gets typed as two sentences within 60 seconds: situation and next action. If you cannot write it in two sentences, the problem is too abstract — that is the symptom, not the problem.
  • Scheduled Thinking Time only: 30 minutes, at a desk, on paper. Problems outside this slot are parked: "I'll work on this at [time]."
  • No problem-spinning in the shower, in transit, or in bed. These are not thinking contexts. If an idea appears, write it in one sentence and move on.

Thinking Time protocol — 30 min, once daily, scheduled

  1. Sit at a desk. Paper and pen only — no screens, no AI.

§7 — Compulsion 6 · Recovery Experiences (Sonnentag) + Stimulus Control

Compulsive Availability Primary · Prevents recovery

What this is

Staying mentally attached to work after the work day ends is not conscientiousness. Bakker, Demerouti, Oerlemans & Sonnentag (2013) found that work-related activity in the evening is specifically more harmful for people with workaholic patterns than for others, while physical and social activities are specifically more protective. You cannot recover by staying available. You recover by fully disengaging.

Being available prevents the disconfirming experience that nothing catastrophic happens when you disconnect. Sonnentag & Fritz's Recovery Experience Questionnaire identifies four protective experiences: psychological detachment, relaxation, mastery, and control — none of which can occur while you remain cognitively attached to work.

§8 — Compulsion 7 · ERP + Theory A/B (Reassurance-Seeking Variant)

AI / Tool Loop Dependence Secondary · Uniquely disguised

What this is

Using AI to "compare framings," "check if this is right," or "generate options before deciding" is functionally identical to asking a person for reassurance. It is not a productivity tool in those moments. It is a machine that never tires of providing relief, never refuses, generates more options (which increases uncertainty, which drives more prompting), and is available at 2am.

AI use outsources the discomfort of making a decision under uncertainty. It externalizes your judgment rather than building tolerance for imperfect judgment. Every time you prompt before deciding, you confirm the meta-belief: "I cannot trust my own assessment." That belief gets stronger, not weaker, with use.

The functional test — write this before every AI use

The Functional Test

What exact deliverable will this produce or change within the next 20 minutes?

If the answer is vague, the behavior is probably serving discomfort regulation, not the task.

Vague answer — stop

  • "It will make the system cleaner."
  • "It will help future me."
  • "It will clarify the whole thing."
Concrete answer — allowed
  • "It will identify one bug in this function."
  • "It will improve the transition in paragraph 3."
  • "It will extract three quotes for this section."
  • "It will decide between option A and B by 14:30."
Hard Rules
  • AI available in one 30-minute window per day. Outside that window, it is logged out. Not minimized — logged out.
  • One-Ask Rule: before any prompt, write in your notebook: (a) the question in one sentence, (b) the feeling you are trying to reduce, (c) is this answerable and one-time? If anxiety-driven — do not submit.
  • Never re-ask the same question with different wording. If you rephrase, you are seeking reassurance, not information. Close the window.
  • Never compare two AI responses. "Let me ask Claude and then check GPT" is the same compulsion in two browser tabs.
  • No AI use for decisions about your own work before you have produced a first version. Produce first. AI may respond to what exists, not replace the first act of producing it.

The core protocol: Output → Support → Stop

  1. Output first: produce a rough version using current tools and current knowledge.
  2. Support second: use AI only to improve the existing output — one bounded question about the thing that now exists.
  3. Stop third: end after one bounded support pass. Do not restart the loop.
Before-output AI — not allowed
  • "Help me figure out what I should write."
  • "Give me the best structure."
  • "Generate options before I decide."
  • "What's the optimal way to approach this?"
  • "Give me 10 possible framings."
  • "Build the whole architecture for me."
After-output AI — allowed
  • "Here is my draft. Identify the main claim."
  • "Here is my email. Flag only ambiguity."
  • "Here is my code. Identify the likely bug — do not rewrite."
  • "Here is my plan. Find one unrealistic assumption."
  • "I chose option A. List one risk to mitigate."

Three AI use rules

Rule A — Draft-first AI

You may use AI only after you have created your own version. Before AI: write the paragraph, choose the option, draft the email, sketch the plan, make the decision, write the code attempt, state your current interpretation. Then AI may comment on what exists.

Rule B — One prompt → one action

Every AI interaction must end in a real-world action, not another prompt.

Old writing loop: Ask for better wording → ask for more academic version → ask for clearer version → compare → lose trust → ask another model.

New practice: Ask "Which sentence is least clear?" → revise that sentence → close AI.


Old coding loop: Ask for architecture → ask for refactor → ask for best practices → redesign the project.

New practice: Ask "What is the smallest failing assumption in this function?" → test that one thing → close AI.

Rule C — No model-shopping

Do not ask multiple AI systems the same question to feel more certain. Model comparison is allowed only if all three conditions are true: (1) the decision is genuinely high-stakes, (2) you write the comparison criteria before asking, (3) you stop after the planned comparison and make a decision.

Concrete scenarios

Scenario 1 — You want AI to choose the best wording

Loop: write one sentence, dislike it, ask AI for alternatives, compare six options, ask for a more nuanced version, lose the original point.

Practice:

  1. Write the sentence yourself.
  2. Mark it [ROUGH] instead of asking AI.
  3. Continue producing for 10 minutes.
  4. After the section exists, ask AI one question: "Which sentence is unclear? Do not rewrite; just point to it."
  5. Fix one sentence. Stop.

Exposure target: tolerate the feeling that your wording is not optimal.

Scenario 2 — You want AI to tell you whether a decision is right

Loop: ask AI which decision is best, ask it to compare criteria, ask another model, feel more confused.

Practice:

  1. Choose the option you would pick if AI were unavailable.
  2. Write: "I choose Option B because ______."
  3. Ask AI only: "Name one practical risk of Option B."
  4. If the risk is not catastrophic, proceed. No second opinion.

Exposure target: tolerate responsibility for a good-enough decision.

Scenario 3 — Coding / debugging loop

Loop: paste errors repeatedly, ask for a rewrite, ask for "best practice," refactor before understanding the bug.

Practice:

  1. Reproduce the bug yourself.
  2. Write one hypothesis.
  3. Ask AI for one test of that hypothesis.
  4. Run the test before asking anything else.
  5. If the test fails, write what changed. Only then ask the next question.

Exposure target: tolerate not knowing while testing sequentially.

Scenario 4 — You want AI to perfect a message to someone

Loop: draft a message, ask AI to make it warmer, then clearer, then less needy, then more assertive — you no longer know what you mean.

Practice:

  1. Write the message in your own words.
  2. Ask: "Is this message clear and respectful? Answer yes/no and name one issue."
  3. Make one edit only.
  4. Send, or schedule sending. No tone-shopping.

Exposure target: tolerate being slightly imperfect and still communicative.

Scenario 5 — Learning / research spiral

Loop: ask AI for an explanation, then a deeper explanation, then sources, then counterarguments, then "what am I missing?" until the task expands.

Practice:

  1. Define the output first: "I need a 5-bullet summary / one paragraph / one decision."
  2. Ask one learning question.
  3. Immediately produce the output from memory.
  4. Check once for accuracy. Stop.

Exposure target: tolerate partial understanding and convert learning into output.

Scenario 6 — You want AI because your body feels anxious

Loop: anxiety spike → open AI → ask for framing → feel relief → more options appear → more anxiety later.

Practice:

  1. Put both feet on the floor. Keep AI closed.
  2. Write in the document: [URGE: want AI because this feels uncertain]
  3. Set a 10-minute timer.
  4. Write one deliberately bad opening: "What I am trying to say is…"
  5. After 10 minutes, rate the urge again.

Exposure target: tolerate the urge without using AI as emotional regulation.

Scenario 7 — You want AI to reassure you about a health concern or mental state

Loop: you describe a symptom, ask AI whether it could be serious, ask again with more detail, add nuance, search, then ask a different tool. The loop never ends because AI cannot give medical certainty and each response opens new uncertainty.

Practice:

  1. Do not use AI for symptom assessment or medical reassurance — ever.
  2. Use a predetermined rule instead:
    — Urgent or acute symptoms → medical service / emergency line, not AI.
    — Non-urgent concern → book a clinician or wait until your next planned appointment.
    — Anxiety spike about health → urge protocol (below), not AI.
  3. Write the concern once in a notebook. One sentence.
  4. No repeated symptom prompting. The note is the action. The appointment (if needed) is the action.

Exposure target: tolerate health uncertainty without digital reassurance. For any genuine clinical concern, the appropriate response is a clinician — not a tool that can only generate more options.

⚡ When the prompting urge hits outside the window

Step 1Write the question in the notebook. That is enough for now.
Step 2Rate the anxiety 0–10. Set a 10-minute timer. Do not open AI.
Step 3Ask: "What is the worst that happens if I make this decision with my own judgment right now?" Write the answer. Then make the decision without prompting.
Step 4Alternative: contact a human and say "I'm anxious about X — I don't need you to fix it, I need company while I sit with it." Support without certainty.

§9 — Compulsion 8 · ACT Workability + Procrastination CBT

§13 — When Everything Collapses

Emergency Protocol

There will be days when every compulsion fires at once and you follow none of the rules. This is not failure — it is an OCD-spectrum pattern under stress, which behaves exactly like this. The protocol for those days is not to restart the entire plan. It is to do one thing.

⚠ Emergency sequence — in order, stop when stable

Do not try to restart the whole plan. Do not redesign the system. Do exactly one step below, stop when the urge subsides, and log what happened.

1Name what is happening. Out loud: "Every compulsion is firing and I am in avoidance mode." This activates prefrontal labeling and reduces amygdala reactivity.
2Do not try to fix everything. Pick the one compulsion with the highest current urge. Apply only that section's protocol. One urge, one intervention.
3Set a 5-minute timer and produce one sentence of the primary deliverable. One sentence. In any condition. This is the behavioral experiment. The specific sentence does not matter.
4Log what happened. What fired. What you resisted. What you did not. One sentence each. This converts the collapse into data.
5Apply the self-compassionate close: "That was a hard day. The plan continues tomorrow." Not to excuse the breach — to prevent the shame spiral that drives the next avoidance cycle.
6Do not rebuild, redesign, or restructure the plan. The urge to redesign the system after a bad day is Compulsion 8 in disguise. The plan stands as written.

One honest caveat

This plan is evidence-based and self-directed. Self-directed ERP is significantly harder than therapist-administered ERP. If these compulsions are materially impairing your functioning — output, relationships, sleep, health — a therapist trained in ERP and OCD-spectrum work would accelerate this considerably. This plan is not a replacement for that; it is the best available without it.

The ADHD-adjacent layer is worth separate attention. If there is genuine ADHD underneath this pattern, that dysregulation can undermine CBT gains — not make them impossible, but harder to sustain without addressing that layer. This is not a reason to delay starting the plan. It is a reason to be honest when the gains plateau.

This is not a diagnosis or a substitute for therapy. Every rule is a rule. When you feel like breaking one, that feeling is the compulsion — not a sign the rule is wrong.
