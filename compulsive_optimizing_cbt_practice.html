Compulsive Optimizing

Concrete practices for the loop of endlessly improving structures, systems, tools, workflows, plans, designs, arguments, notes, prompts, routines, or environments instead of moving the actual work forward.

1. What compulsive optimizing is

Compulsive optimizing is the repeated urge to improve the conditions, structure, wording, system, tool, process, or plan before acting. The behavior often looks intelligent and responsible. That is why it is sticky.

Core definition: compulsive optimizing is not “liking things to be good.” It is the pattern where improving the setup repeatedly delays, displaces, or dilutes the actual output.

Healthy optimization

  • Solves a specific bottleneck.
  • Has a clear stopping point.
  • Leads directly to an output.
  • Is proportionate to the task.
  • Can be postponed without panic.

Compulsive optimization

  • Expands as you work on it.
  • Feels urgent even when not necessary.
  • Delays the real task.
  • Creates temporary relief.
  • Returns quickly: “just one more improvement.”

Common forms beyond academia

Context Compulsive optimizing may look like Actual output being avoided
Thesis / academic writing Reworking theme names, chapter structure, note categories, literature maps. Writing rough prose and making provisional claims.
Work project Rebuilding the plan, slide structure, project board, documentation format. Sending the draft, making the decision, shipping the deliverable.
Software / coding Refactoring architecture, improving naming, changing frameworks, adding abstractions. Implementing the feature, testing the bug fix, releasing the version.
Personal productivity Switching apps, redesigning tags, building dashboards, changing routines. Doing the unpleasant task already on the list.
Health / habits Creating the perfect workout, diet plan, tracker, supplement stack. Doing today’s workout or cooking the next meal.
Creative work Choosing tools, templates, palettes, structure, references, aesthetic direction. Making the draft, sketch, scene, page, prototype, or recording.

2. The CBT formulation

In CBT terms, compulsive optimizing usually functions as a combination of perfectionistic safety behavior, avoidance, and short-term anxiety regulation. It is maintained because it gives relief now while preventing you from learning that imperfect action is survivable.

Trigger:
“This is not good enough / not clear enough / not ready.”

Threat prediction:
“If I act now, the result will be bad, judged, inefficient, wrong, embarrassing, or wasteful.”

Safety behavior:
Optimize the structure, tool, wording, plan, system, workflow, or setup.

Short-term effect:
Relief, control, stimulation, feeling of progress.

Long-term effect:
Less tolerance for imperfect action, more dependence on optimizing, less output.

CBT target: not to stop improving things forever. The target is to stop using optimization as the gatekeeper before action.

3. The practical rule set

These are not slogans. They are operational rules. Choose one or two to start; do not implement all at once.

Rule 1
Output before optimization
Produce a rough output before improving the system around it. Example: write the paragraph before renaming the theme; ship the draft before redesigning the template; complete the workout before optimizing the training plan.
Rule 2
Optimization must have a ticket
You may optimize only if you can write a specific problem ticket: “This exact bottleneck prevented this exact output.” Vague tickets like “make the system better” are not allowed.
Rule 3
Time-box, then act
Give optimization a short box: 5, 10, or 15 minutes. When the timer ends, act with the current version. No emotional readiness required.
Rule 4
No mid-flight redesign
Once a production block starts, no changing the structure, tool, framework, theme name, template, or plan. Capture ideas in brackets or on paper. Keep producing.
Rule 5
Revise from output
You are allowed to improve the structure after a draft exists. The draft becomes evidence. Before the draft, most optimization is speculation.
Rule 6
Use “good enough” criteria
Define “good enough” behaviorally before starting. Do not use feelings as the finish line.

4. Good-enough criteria: concrete examples

Compulsive optimizing survives because “done” is emotional and movable. Use behavioral criteria instead.

Task Bad completion criterion Better behavioral criterion
Thesis paragraph “It feels clear and theoretically right.” One claim, one explanation, one piece of evidence or placeholder, one next-step note.
Project plan “The whole system feels organized.” Owner, next action, deadline, and first deliverable are specified.
Code feature “The architecture feels clean.” Feature works for the main case, has one test, and known cleanup is listed.
Email / message “It sounds exactly right.” Recipient, request, context, deadline, and one clear next step are included.
Workout plan “This is the optimal program.” Today’s workout is chosen and completed at 70% adequacy.
Note system “Everything is categorized elegantly.” The note helps complete one current output today.

Important: good-enough criteria should feel slightly uncomfortable. If they feel fully satisfying, they may still be perfectionistic criteria in disguise.

5. In-the-moment protocol

Use this when you are already inside the urge to optimize. The protocol does not ask you to convince yourself. It gives your body something specific to do.

The 10-minute anti-optimization sequence

  1. Freeze the optimization. Hands off the outline, template, tool, theme name, plan, code structure, or system.
  2. Write the urge as a ticket. One sentence only: “I want to optimize ___ because I fear ___.”
  3. Defer it visibly. Put it in a “Later / Optimization Parking Lot.”
  4. Choose the smallest output action. One sentence, one line of code, one paragraph stub, one email draft, one messy slide.
  5. Work for 10 minutes with optimization blocked. Timer on. No structural changes.
  6. Re-rate the urge. 0–10 before and after.
  7. Decide from evidence. After output exists, ask whether the optimization is still necessary.
Optimization Parking Lot

Urge:
I want to rename/restructure/rebuild/improve __________.

Fear:
If I do not optimize it now, __________ will happen.

Deferred until:
After 25 minutes of output / Friday review / after draft exists.

Still necessary after output exists?   Yes / No / Smaller version

6. Exposure exercises

These are deliberate practice exercises. They work by letting you experience the discomfort of not optimizing and discovering that action remains possible.

Level 1

Write under the imperfect label

Use when: you want to rename a theme, heading, folder, project, section, or file before working.

Exposure:
Keep the current name for 25 minutes.
Produce output under that name.

Example:
Keep “Theme 2: Institutional Friction” even if you dislike it.
Write 250 words below it.

Response prevention:
No renaming, no reordering, no taxonomy redesign.
Level 2

Use the current tool badly

Use when: you want to improve a tool, template, note system, automation, or integration before starting.

Exposure:
Use the current imperfect tool for one output block.

Example:
Use the existing note document, even if messy.
Write the paragraph anyway.

Response prevention:
No new template, no integration, no cleanup, no automation.
Level 3

Ship the 70% version

Use when: you want to keep polishing something that is already functional.

Exposure:
Send/share/save/submit the 70% version to a low-risk audience or checkpoint.

Example:
Send supervisor a rough theme memo labeled “rough analytic direction.”
Send colleague a draft plan with known gaps.

Response prevention:
No final polish pass beyond one timed 10-minute cleanup.
Level 4

Leave the visible imperfection

Use when: the urge is to fix cosmetic or structural discomfort immediately.

Exposure:
Leave one non-dangerous imperfection visible until the next scheduled review.

Examples:
A clunky heading.
An awkward paragraph.
A rough file name.
A nonideal slide order.
A code TODO.

Response prevention:
No immediate cleanup unless it blocks the actual output.
Level 5

Decide without comparing all options

Use when: you keep comparing frameworks, tools, outlines, headings, apps, or approaches.

Exposure:
Compare only two options.
Choose one.
Use it for 48 hours.

Response prevention:
No third option search.
No “just checking alternatives.”
No asking AI for more possibilities.

7. Worked examples

Example A: thesis theme names

Situation: You want to rename the theme because the current name feels clumsy.

Old behavior: spend 45 minutes generating alternatives, asking AI, reorganizing subthemes, then feel too drained to write.

New behavior:

1. Write: “I want to rename this theme because I fear the current name misrepresents the finding.”
2. Add bracket: [Possible rename later]
3. Lock current name for 25 minutes.
4. Write 250 words under the current name.
5. After writing, ask: did the name actually block the paragraph?
6. If still needed, schedule a 10-minute naming review after prose exists.

Example B: productivity system redesign

Situation: You feel your notes are messy and want to build a better dashboard.

Old behavior: open Notion/Obsidian/Sheets, restructure databases, add tags, create views, then call it progress.

New behavior:

1. Write a tool ticket:
   “I want to redesign the dashboard because I fear I cannot see what to do next.”

2. Test the belief:
   Can I define the next action without redesigning?

3. Choose next output:
   “Write 200 words explaining finding X.”

4. Work 25 minutes using current notes.

5. Only if the messy notes directly prevented output, define a tiny fix:
   “Create one quote list for Theme 2.”
   Not: “rebuild the whole system.”

Example C: code/project architecture

Situation: You are building something and want to refactor before completing the feature.

Old behavior: reorganize architecture, rename modules, improve abstractions, then never test the actual feature.

New behavior:

1. Define output:
   “Main user path works once.”

2. Lock architecture for 45 minutes.

3. Add TODOs instead of refactoring:
   // TODO: rename this after feature works
   // TODO: extract helper later

4. Complete the feature path.

5. Refactor only after:
   - feature runs
   - one test exists
   - specific pain point is demonstrated

Example D: email/message wording

Situation: You repeatedly rewrite a message because the tone is not perfect.

Old behavior: edit for 30 minutes, ask AI for alternate versions, compare phrasing, delay sending.

New behavior:

Good-enough criteria:
- clear recipient
- clear ask
- necessary context
- deadline or next step
- polite enough

Rule:
One draft.
One 3-minute edit.
Send.

Exposure:
Send a polite but imperfect message and record whether the feared outcome happened.

9. Stopping rules

Stopping rules are essential because compulsive optimizing has no natural endpoint.

Situation Stopping rule What to do next
Renaming / headings / labels Pick one name in 5 minutes. Lock for 48 hours. Write under it.
Outline / structure Maximum 10 minutes before drafting. Draft one subsection before any further changes.
Tool or app choice Use current tool for 7 days unless it truly blocks output. Produce with current setup.
Prompt engineering / AI workflow One prompt only after your own draft exists. Apply one useful suggestion or close AI.
Design / format / aesthetics One 10-minute formatting pass after content exists. Return to substance or stop.
Refactoring / cleanup Only after the core function works. Make a TODO and complete the feature.
Planning Planning must end with one next action. Do that action immediately.

10. Handling the physical-emotional urge

The urge to optimize often arrives as bodily discomfort: tension, pressure, agitation, dread, heat, restlessness, or a sharp feeling that something is “wrong.” The intervention is behavioral: carry the discomfort while doing the next output action.

Do not wait for the urge to disappear. If you wait to feel settled before acting, the urge has already become the gatekeeper.

The body-based sequence

  1. Pause hands. Stop touching the thing you want to optimize.
  2. Name the body sensation. “Tight chest,” “buzzing,” “heat,” “pressure,” “restlessness.”
  3. Rate intensity. 0–10.
  4. Make room physically. Unclench jaw, lower shoulders, exhale longer than inhale for three breaths.
  5. Do not negotiate with the urge. No debating whether optimization is justified for the next 10 minutes.
  6. Perform one output action. Type the next sentence, line, claim, paragraph stub, test, email sentence, or sketch.
  7. Re-rate after 10 minutes. The goal is not zero. The goal is movement without compulsion.
When urge intensity is 7/10 or lower:
Continue the exposure.

When urge intensity is 8–9/10:
Shrink the task, not the rule.
Example: one sentence instead of one paragraph.

When distress feels unsafe or overwhelming:
Stop the exposure, use grounding, and discuss pacing with a therapist.
Do not use this guide as a substitute for clinical care.

11. Permissions that produce behavior

These are not affirmations. Each permission is paired with an action.

Permission Immediate action
I am allowed to use an imperfect structure. Write 150 words under the current heading.
I am allowed to leave a clunky label unchanged. Add [rename later] and continue the paragraph.
I am allowed to work with a messy tool. Use the current note system for one 25-minute block.
I am allowed to decide with incomplete comparison. Compare two options only, choose one, use for 48 hours.
I am allowed to make a reversible mistake. Publish/send/share/save the low-risk 70% version.
I am allowed to optimize later. Put the idea in the parking lot and produce first.

12. A seven-day practice plan

This plan is intentionally narrow. The objective is not to fix all optimization. The objective is to gather evidence that you can act before optimizing.

Day 1 — Label lock

Pick one imperfect label, heading, folder, or section name. Keep it for 25 minutes and produce under it.

Day 2 — Tool freeze

Use the current tool or setup for one output block. No fixes, tags, templates, integrations, or cleanup.

Day 3 — 70% output

Produce a low-risk 70% version. Send, save, share, or mark complete with one known imperfection.

Day 4 — Two-option decision

Choose between only two structures/tools/wordings. Use the chosen one for 48 hours.

Day 5 — No mid-flight redesign

Start a 25-minute block. Capture every optimization idea in brackets. Implement none.

Day 6 — Deferred optimization

Delay one optimization urge for 24 hours. Check whether it still matters tomorrow.

Day 7 — Review evidence

Review predictions vs outcomes. Do not redesign the practice plan. Choose one rule to repeat next week.

14. Red flags: when optimization may be justified

The goal is not reckless action. Some optimization is legitimate. Use this checklist.

  • The current setup creates a real safety, legal, ethical, or privacy risk.
  • The output cannot proceed at all without the fix.
  • The same specific bottleneck has blocked output more than once.
  • The fix can be done in under 15 minutes and leads immediately back to output.
  • The optimization is scheduled, bounded, and not performed during an urge spike.

If only one item is true, be cautious. If the main reason is “I’ll feel better once this is cleaner,” it is probably the compulsion, not the task.

15. The shortest usable version

Use this when you are in the middle of the loop and cannot think clearly.

1. Stop optimizing.
2. Write the fear:
   “If I do not optimize ___, then ___.”
3. Put the optimization idea in the parking lot.
4. Set 10 minutes.
5. Produce one imperfect piece of output.
6. Re-check whether optimization is still necessary after output exists.

This guide is educational and practical, not a diagnosis or substitute for therapy. It is designed to be adapted with a therapist, especially if distress is severe, impairment is high, or compulsive patterns feel uncontrollable.
