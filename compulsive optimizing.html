CBT Practice Protocol: Compulsive Optimizing

Target pattern: compulsive optimizing in thesis writing.

Thesis-specific form: reworking themes, headings, outlines, chapter architecture, note systems, coding structures, labels, workflows, and conceptual frameworks instead of producing prose.

Main CBT frame: perfectionistic overcontrol plus avoidance of imperfect commitment.

Primary behavioral target: write usable-but-imperfect prose under a provisional structure, without changing the structure during the writing block.

1. What compulsive optimizing looks like in your thesis

Compulsive optimizing is not ordinary revision. Revision happens after text exists. Compulsive optimizing happens before or during writing and repeatedly changes the conditions for writing.

Surface behavior What it looks like in thesis work Why it feels justified What it may actually be doing
Renaming themes Changing “institutional friction” to “structural constraint,” then “professional negotiation,” then “institutional positioning.” The exact wording matters. Avoiding writing the actual finding under any one label.
Reordering sections Moving Theme 2 before Theme 1, then reversing it, then creating a new bridge section. The chapter needs a logical flow. Avoiding the discomfort of drafting under an imperfect flow.
Reworking headings Spending 40 minutes on headings before writing any paragraph. Headings clarify the argument. Trying to feel oriented before making a claim.
Changing the coding/theme system Reclassifying excerpts, merging themes, splitting categories, revisiting old analytic decisions. The analysis must be rigorous. Returning to analysis to avoid committing to findings prose.
Improving the workflow Changing note layouts, summary formats, quote tables, tagging systems, or AI prompts. Better infrastructure will make writing easier. Improving the runway instead of taking off.

Important distinction: optimizing is not always bad. It becomes compulsive when it repeatedly happens before prose, interrupts prose, or prevents a stable draft object from emerging.

2. The core trap

The trap is the belief that the structure must feel right before writing can begin. 

Hidden rule:
“I must find the correct structure before I write.”

CBT counter-practice:
“I will write under a provisional structure and let prose reveal what needs changing.”

The practical goal is not to convince yourself intellectually. The goal is to run repeated behavioral experiments where you write under imperfect structures and observe that the feared catastrophe does not occur.

3. Difference between real structuring and compulsive optimizing

Real structuring Compulsive optimizing
Has a time limit. Expands until it feels right.
Produces a decision. Produces more possible decisions.
Supports a specific paragraph or section. Improves the whole system generally.
Happens before a planned writing block, briefly. Interrupts the writing block.
Ends with “good enough for now.” Ends only when anxiety drops.
Moves from structure to prose. Moves from structure to more structure.

4. The main intervention: structure lock

Structure lock means that for a defined writing block, the current theme name, heading, outline order, and section position are frozen.

You may notice problems. You may write them down in brackets. You may not implement changes during the block.

Basic structure-lock rule

Before the block:
5 minutes maximum to choose the current structure.

During the block:
No renaming.
No reordering.
No moving sections.
No revising the outline.
No changing the theme architecture.
No editing the note system.
No opening a new planning document.

Allowed:
Bracketed notes only.

Example:
[STRUCTURE NOTE: maybe this belongs under Theme 3 — review later]

5. Concrete practice block: “Write under the imperfect heading”

This is the first practical exercise. It directly targets the compulsion to fix the structure before writing.

Exercise A: 25-minute imperfect-heading block

Step 1 — Choose one current heading.

Pick the heading as it currently exists. Do not improve it.

Example current heading: “Institutional friction”

Step 2 — Write the feared prediction. 
If I write under this imperfect heading, then the section will become confused and unusable.
Step 3 — Set a 25-minute timer.

During the timer, the heading is locked. Even if a better title appears, you cannot change it.

Step 4 — Write 150–300 rough words under that heading.

Your only task is to make the current finding visible in prose.

Step 5 — Bracket structure urges instead of obeying them. 
[STRUCTURE NOTE: this may need to be reframed as “navigating institutional expectations” later]
Step 6 — After the timer, answer only outcome questions. 
Was the paragraph actually unusable?
Did writing under the imperfect heading create new information?
Did the better structure become clearer after prose existed?

Filled example

Heading locked: Institutional friction

Feared prediction: If I write under “Institutional friction,” I will flatten the theme and miss the identity dimension.

25-minute output:

Participants did not describe institutional barriers only as external obstacles. Rather, these barriers appeared to shape how they understood their own professional agency. In several accounts, institutional rules, unclear expectations, and inconsistent support created moments where participants had to interpret what kind of professional they were allowed or expected to become. [QUOTE HERE] This suggests that “friction” was not merely practical but interpretive: it forced participants to negotiate their role in relation to the institution.

After-block review:

  • The paragraph was not unusable.
  • The identity dimension appeared inside the prose.
  • A better heading might be “Institutional friction and professional agency,” but this became clearer after writing, not before.

6. Concrete practice block: “No-renaming exposure”

This exercise targets compulsive theme renaming.

Exercise B: 48-hour theme-name lock

Purpose: practice tolerating an imperfect theme name long enough to produce substance.

Rule:
For 48 hours, I cannot rename the theme.
I can collect possible alternative names in a small list.
I cannot implement them until the lock expires.

How to do it

  1. Choose the current theme name.
  2. Write it at the top of the section.
  3. Under it, write at least two prose blocks across the next 48 hours.
  4. Every renaming urge goes into a “name candidates” list.
  5. At the end of 48 hours, review whether renaming is still needed.

Name candidates list

Current locked name:
Institutional friction

Possible later names:
- Institutional friction and professional agency
- Negotiating institutional expectations
- Institutional constraint as identity work
- Friction as professional positioning

Review date/time:
Thursday, 17:00

What this trains

  • You learn that a theme name does not need to be final before prose can develop.
  • You learn that some “urgent” renaming ideas become irrelevant after writing.
  • You separate idea capture from compulsive implementation.

7. Concrete practice block: “Outline freeze and prose-first revision”

This exercise targets repeated outline restructuring.

Exercise C: 3-block outline freeze

Purpose: prevent repeated restructuring before enough prose exists to evaluate the structure.

Rule:
The outline is frozen for three writing blocks.
Each block must produce prose.
Only after three blocks may I review the outline.

Example frozen outline

Findings Chapter

1. Introduction to findings
2. Theme 1: Institutional friction
3. Theme 2: Negotiated agency
4. Theme 3: Relational support
5. Cross-theme tension
6. Chapter summary

During the three blocks

Block Task Forbidden Allowed
Block 1 Write 250 words under Theme 1. Moving Theme 1 elsewhere. [Maybe Theme 1 should come after Theme 2]
Block 2 Write 250 words under Theme 2. Renaming both themes. [Possible overlap with Theme 1]
Block 3 Write 250 words under Theme 3 or cross-theme tension. Creating a new chapter architecture. [Need transition between Theme 2 and 3]

Only after three blocks

Review:
What did the prose reveal?
Which structure issue is real?
Which structure issue was anxiety?
What is the smallest necessary change?

8. Concrete practice block: “Optimization parking lot”

This exercise separates noticing an optimization idea from acting on it.

Exercise D: Optimization parking lot

Keep a simple section at the bottom of the draft or on paper.

OPTIMIZATION PARKING LOT

Date/time:
Optimization urge:
What triggered it:
Would acting now produce prose? Yes/No
Parked action:
Review time:

Filled example

Date/time:
Tuesday, 11:20

Optimization urge:
Rename Theme 2 and move it before Theme 1.

What triggered it:
While writing paragraph 2, I felt that the argument order might be wrong.

Would acting now produce prose?
No. It would interrupt the writing block.

Parked action:
Review after 3 prose blocks.

Review time:
Friday, 12:00

Practical rule

If an optimization idea appears during drafting:
Capture it in one sentence.
Return to the paragraph.
Do not implement.

9. Concrete practice block: “Deliberately imperfect version”

This exercise targets the need to make a section conceptually elegant before it exists.

Exercise E: Version 0.4 draft

Purpose: reduce the pressure to produce polished, elegant academic prose.

Rule:
The next section is explicitly labeled Version 0.4.
Version 0.4 is not allowed to be elegant.
It only needs to contain the raw argument.

Version 0.4 requirements

  • One rough claim.
  • One example or quote placeholder.
  • One sentence explaining why it matters.
  • One bracketed uncertainty.

Example Version 0.4 section

Version 0.4 — Theme 1: Institutional friction

Rough claim:
Participants describe institutional friction not only as a barrier but as something that shapes how they understand their professional role.

Evidence:
[Insert quote from Participant 3 about unclear institutional expectations]

Why it matters:
This matters because the finding shows that professional identity is not only internally developed but negotiated in relation to institutional conditions.

Uncertainty:
[Need to check whether this belongs before or after negotiated agency]

11. In-the-moment protocol when optimizing urge hits

This is not a mantra. It is an action sequence.

Protocol: Notice → Park → Continue

  1. Stop typing for 5 seconds.
  2. Name the behavior: “This is an optimizing urge.”
  3. Write one bracketed note: [OPTIMIZE LATER: ...]
  4. Set a 10-minute mini-timer.
  5. Continue the current paragraph until the timer ends.
  6. At 10 minutes, do not optimize yet. Choose either another 10 minutes or finish the sentence/paragraph.

Example in action

Moment: You are writing under “Institutional friction” and suddenly think, “This is really about agency, not friction.”

Old behavior: rename the theme, move paragraphs, revise the outline, ask AI for theme names.

New behavior:

[OPTIMIZE LATER: this may be more about agency than friction]

Continue sentence:
This suggests that participants experienced institutional demands not simply as external constraints, but as conditions through which they interpreted their own professional agency.

Result: The optimization idea is preserved, but it no longer controls the writing block.

12. Stopping rules for optimization

These rules are designed to prevent optimization from expanding.

Situation Stopping rule Example
Choosing a theme name Maximum 3 candidates, then choose one for 48 hours. Pick “Institutional friction” even if “Negotiated agency” might be better.
Changing outline order No outline changes until three prose blocks exist. Write under Theme 1, Theme 2, Theme 3 before moving them.
Revising headings Headings can be edited only at the end of the day, not during drafting. Collect heading ideas in brackets; review at 17:00.
Improving systems No system changes during thesis writing blocks. Do not alter note templates while drafting findings.
Feeling the structure is wrong Write one paragraph that demonstrates the problem before changing the structure. Instead of moving a section, write: “This may belong later because...”
Wanting AI to optimize structure AI may review existing prose, not generate structure before prose. Ask “What is the claim in this paragraph?” not “What should my themes be?”

13. Permissions that are behavioral, not just verbal

Permissions are useful only when tied to specific allowed actions.

Old forbidden experience New permission Concrete action
The heading feels wrong. I can write under a wrong heading temporarily. Write 200 words without renaming it.
The theme order feels uncertain. I can test the order through prose. Draft one paragraph for each theme before moving them.
The structure feels inelegant. Elegance is not required at draft stage. Label the section Version 0.4 and write the raw claim.
I see a better system. System ideas can wait. Put the idea in the parking lot and continue the sentence.
I am afraid of wasting time. Writing rough prose is not wasted time. Run a 25-minute test and review output afterward.

14. A practical exposure hierarchy for compulsive optimizing

Start with lower-intensity exposures. Do not begin with the hardest one.

Level Exposure Duration Response prevention
1 — Low Write 100 words under a slightly imperfect heading. 10 minutes No renaming during the 10 minutes.
2 — Low-medium Write 200 words under the current theme name. 25 minutes No heading edits; bracket ideas only.
3 — Medium Complete one mini-section using the current outline. 45 minutes No reordering sections.
4 — Medium-high Use the current three-theme structure for three writing blocks. Three blocks No merging/splitting themes until all three blocks are done.
5 — High Send a rough section to supervisor/therapist/peer with imperfect headings. One send action No final structural overhaul before sending.
6 — High Submit a chapter draft with bracketed structure notes still present. One submission No “perfect structure” pass before submission.

15. Realistic thesis scenarios and exact responses

Scenario 1: You want to rename a theme mid-paragraph

Old response: Stop writing, open outline, brainstorm better theme names, ask AI for alternatives, compare names, lose 45 minutes.

New response:

1. Type: [NAME IDEA: maybe “negotiated agency”]
2. Continue the paragraph under the old name.
3. Review name ideas only after the block.
4. If the new name still seems useful after 48 hours, change it then.

Scenario 2: You realize two themes overlap

Old response: Merge themes immediately, rewrite outline, recategorize quotes, redesign findings structure.

New response:

1. Write: [OVERLAP NOTE: Theme 1 and Theme 2 may overlap around agency]
2. Finish the current subsection.
3. Write one paragraph for each theme before deciding whether to merge.
4. Decide based on prose, not anxiety.

Scenario 3: The chapter order suddenly feels wrong

Old response: Move sections, reread chapter, re-plan entire findings arc.

New response:

1. Keep the order frozen.
2. Write a transition placeholder:
   [TRANSITION NEEDED: explain why Theme 2 follows Theme 1]
3. Continue drafting.
4. Review order only after three new paragraphs exist.

Scenario 4: You think your note system is causing the problem

Old response: Rebuild note templates, change summary format, create better quote extraction, improve tagging.

New response:

1. Write with the current note system for 25 minutes.
2. If blocked, write exactly what was missing:
   “I could not find quotes for Theme 2.”
3. Only after the writing block, decide if a small tool fix is needed.
4. Maximum fix: 15 minutes, one specific bottleneck.

16. Daily practice plan for one week

Day Practice Rule Output
Day 1 Write under an imperfect heading. No renaming for 25 minutes. 150–300 words.
Day 2 Theme-name lock. Use current theme name for the full block. One rough claim paragraph.
Day 3 Outline freeze. No moving sections. One paragraph in the current section order.
Day 4 Minimum viable structure. Use the simple theme template only. Filled template plus one paragraph.
Day 5 Optimization parking lot. Capture all optimization urges; implement none. One uninterrupted 25-minute block.
Day 6 Version 0.4 draft. Write deliberately rough prose. One mini-section with bracketed gaps.
Day 7 Review only. No rewriting during review. List which optimization ideas still matter.

17. Measurement: what to track

Track behavior, not feelings alone. The point is to gather evidence. 

Date:
Writing target:

Optimization urge:
Rename / reorder / restructure / system change / heading change / theme merge / other

Feared prediction:
If I do not optimize, then...

Response prevention used:
Structure lock / parking lot / 48-hour name lock / outline freeze / minimum viable structure

Peak discomfort, 0–10:

Prose produced:

Did the feared outcome happen?

Was the optimization still necessary after the block?

What did writing reveal?

18. Review questions that are actually practical

These questions should be answered after the block, not during the block.

Question Purpose
How many words or paragraphs existed before optimization? Checks whether optimization displaced production.
Did the optimization idea still matter after 25 minutes? Tests whether the urge was urgent or temporary.
Did writing clarify the structure better than thinking did? Tests the belief that structure must precede prose.
Was the produced prose unusable or merely rough? Tests catastrophic predictions.
What was the smallest actual structure change needed? Prevents global restructuring.

19. What not to do

Do not turn this into another optimization project.

  • Do not build a perfect CBT tracker.
  • Do not design a complex scoring system.
  • Do not ask AI to optimize your anti-optimization protocol.
  • Do not spend a week refining the exposure hierarchy.
  • Do not create a dashboard for optimization urges.

Use one plain log. Run one exposure. Review the result.

20. The simplest version

When the full protocol feels too much, use this: 

1. Pick the current heading.
2. Lock it for 25 minutes.
3. Write 200 rough words under it.
4. Put all improvement ideas in brackets.
5. Do not implement them during the block.
6. After the block, ask:
   “Was the writing actually harmed by waiting?”

Core behavioral learning:

You do not need the right structure before writing. You need a temporary structure stable enough to write under. The real structure can be revised from prose.
